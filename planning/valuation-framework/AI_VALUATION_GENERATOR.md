### Calculate Bounds:

**Method 1 (Standard Deviation):**
```
Calculate standard deviation of 9 method valuations
Low Bound = Weighted Average - (0.5 × Std Dev)
High Bound = Weighted Average + (0.5 × Std Dev)
```

**Method 2 (Percentile):**
```
Low Bound = Average of 3 lowest method valuations
High Bound = Average of 3 highest method valuations
```

### Calculate Pre-Money:
```
Pre-Money Valuation = Post-Money Valuation - Capital Needed
                    = $[POST_MONEY] - $[CAPITAL_NEEDED]
                    = $[PRE_MONEY]
```

### Calculate Equity Percentages:
```
Median Equity % = Capital Needed / Post-Money × 100
Low Equity % = Capital Needed / High Bound × 100
High Equity % = Capital Needed / Low Bound × 100
```

## 8.1 Simple VC Method

### Write Introduction:
```
The Venture Capital (VC) method is a widely-used approach among financial practitioners for valuing private companies, particularly startups. This method provides a valuation that ensures investors can achieve a predetermined return upon exit. The VC method is favored for its ability to align the potential exit value of a company with the risk profile and return expectations of venture capital investors, making it a key tool in the assessment of early-stage companies.

Once the exit value is determined, it is discounted back to the present value using a required rate of return (ROI). This discount rate is crucial, as it accounts for the time value of money and the high level of risk associated with early-stage investments. The required ROI is typically higher for early-stage companies, which are riskier and less predictable, and lower for more mature companies with established revenue streams.
```

# STEP 11: SECTION 9.0 - DCF METHOD

## 9.1 DCF with Growth (Weight: 20%)

### Write Introduction:
```
The Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) method with growth is one of the most respected and widely used approaches for valuing companies, favored by academics and financial analysts alike. This method calculates a company's valuation as the present value of all future free cash flows to equity, taking into account the risks associated with the startup's industry, size, development stage, and profitability. By incorporating projected growth into the cash flows, this method provides a forward-looking estimate of a company's value, making it particularly useful for startups with significant growth potential.

**REASONING & ASSUMPTIONS**

The DCF method with growth assumes that future cash flows can be reasonably projected based on the startup's current performance and growth potential. It also assumes that these cash flows can be discounted back to present value using a rate that accurately reflects the startup's risk profile, including industry, size, and stage of development.

Key assumptions include the accuracy of cash flow projections, the appropriateness of the discount rate, and the method used to calculate the Terminal Value. The method also assumes that the illiquidity discount applied is sufficient to reflect the challenges of selling shares in a startup, which may not have a readily available market.
```

### Determine Discount Rate:

**Option 1: Build-up Method**
```
Risk-Free Rate:           [2-4]%
+ Equity Risk Premium:    [5-7]%
+ Size Premium:           [3-6]%
+ Industry Risk:          [2-5]%
+ Company-Specific Risk:  [5-15]%
= DISCOUNT RATE:          [20-35]%
```

**Option 2: Stage-Based (Simpler)**

|-------|---------------|

| Seed | 35-50% |
| Series A | 25-35% |

### Calculate Present Value of Cash Flows:

```
Year 1: $[F1] / (1 + [r])^1 = $[PV1]
Year 2: $[F2] / (1 + [r])^2 = $[PV2]
Year 3: $[F3] / (1 + [r])^3 = $[PV3]
Year 4: $[F4] / (1 + [r])^4 = $[PV4]
Year 5: $[F5] / (1 + [r])^5 = $[PV5]

                 = $[PV_TOTAL]
```

### Calculate Terminal Value:

```

               = $[TV]

PV of Terminal Value = $[TV] / (1 + [r])^5
                     = $[PV_TV]
```

### Calculate DCF Valuation:

```

                          = $[PV_TOTAL] + $[PV_TV]
                          = $[DCF_GROWTH_VAL]
```
