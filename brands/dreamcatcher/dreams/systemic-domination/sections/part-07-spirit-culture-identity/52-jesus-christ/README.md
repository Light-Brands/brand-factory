# Jesus Christ -- From Light of the World to Symbol on the Cross

_Part of the [Systemic Domination](../../../README.md) thesis._

## Content

One of the strangest distortions in religious culture is how Jesus of Nazareth, a teacher of love and light, is most commonly represented by the image of his brutal death. The central symbol of Christianity became the cross – an instrument of execution – with Jesus nailed upon it. Believers wear crucifix necklaces, churches display Jesus on the cross, and for many the crucifixion (Good Friday) is emphasized as much as, or more than, the resurrection (Easter Sunday). Yet this focus was not always the case. In early Christianity, the cross was not a proud symbol at all – in fact, most scholars believe the first Christians did not use the cross image, because crucifixion was a shameful punishmentbiblicalarchaeology.org. It took some 300–400 years after Jesus's time for the crucifix to emerge publicly in Christian art and worship biblicalarchaeology.org. The very first known depiction of Jesus on a cross appears only in the 5th century. Prior to that, Christians used symbols like the fish or anchor, emphasizing fellowship and hope rather than torture. This historical fact begs the question: Why did later Christianity elevate the crucifixion to such prominence? And what does it mean for how we remember Jesus?

Jesus's actual ministry was focused on life, compassion, and enlightenment. He spoke of the "Kingdom of God" – a transformed world of justice and peace – and taught people to love their neighbors, to forgive, to care for the least among them. He said "I am the light of the world" (John 8:12), indicating his role was to bring spiritual light and truth. Notably, during his life Jesus did not teach a "cult of death" or dwell morbidly on the cross. As one commentary points out, "While Jesus was very well aware of where his life was headed, he didn't engage in cross-focused living. He didn't scare people into repentance… [the crucifixion] was just the necessary step on the path to the resurrection so that we can live resurrection-focused lives."benclapton.com In other words, Jesus emphasized living in a way filled with love, forgiveness, and mercy – the values of the resurrected life, not obsession with the agony of death. Early Christians, too, were far more enamored with the resurrection – the triumph of life over death – than with the act of dying. The Apostle Paul wrote, "if Christ has not been raised, our faith is in vain," underscoring that the ultimate message was one of hope, new life, and spiritual victory.

How, then, did Jesus end up eternally depicted in many churches as a suffering figure nailed to wood? There is a line of thought that this serves an agenda of guilt and control. A Jesus radiant on a pedestal of light and peace – an image of pure transcendence – would inspire admiration and empowerment. But a Jesus contorted in pain, bleeding for the sins of humanity, tends to inspire guilt, fear, and a sense of indebtedness. For centuries, the Church institution taught that Jesus had to die for our wickedness, and that believers must constantly remember that sacrifice (often mediated by the Church's authority). The crucifix thus became a symbol of sacrifice and suffering – reinforcing doctrines that humans are hopeless sinners redeemed only by obeying Church teachings. This emphasis can psychologically bind followers to the religion through fear of betraying Jesus's sacrifice. A more positive celebration – say, Jesus as a teacher of enlightenment or as the risen Christ triumphant – did not serve the same institutional purpose. Indeed, some Christian groups (e.g. certain Protestant denominations) have opted to display an empty cross or emphasize the resurrection, precisely to focus on joy and salvation rather than torment. But the predominant imagery, especially in Catholic and Orthodox traditions, remains the crucified Christ. From a truth-seeking perspective, one must ask: is this constant visual of Jesus's execution truly what he intended as his legacy? Or is it a distortion that overshadows his life and message of love? By nailing Jesus to the cross in iconography for millennia, the powers of the time fixed the collective memory on his death rather than his teachings. It is a potent reminder that even sacred narratives can be spun – possibly to keep believers humble, obedient, and in a mindset of needing salvation (as provided by the Church), rather than empowered by the radical example of Jesus's life and enlightenment. Recognizing this, some faithful now try to balance the narrative: honoring the crucifixion, yes, but not as a morbid fetish – rather as one part of a greater story where light ultimately overcomes darkness.
