## Content

The global energy system is not a market responding to demand. It is a monopoly architecture maintained through subsidies, geopolitical force, and the active suppression of alternatives. The International Monetary Fund calculated that fossil fuel subsidies reached $5.9 trillion in 2020, a figure so large it defies comprehension, representing more than the entire GDP of Japan. These subsidies do not exist because fossil fuels are the best available technology. They exist because the fossil fuel industry has captured the regulatory and political infrastructure that determines how civilization is powered. The petrodollar system, established through the Nixon-Saudi agreement of 1974, ensured that global oil transactions would be denominated in US dollars, binding the world's energy supply to American monetary hegemony and giving the United States a strategic interest in perpetuating oil dependency regardless of environmental or human cost. Every war in the Middle East since then must be understood within this framework: the defense of the petrodollar is the defense of empire.

The suppression of alternative energy technologies carries a documented history that the mainstream narrative treats as conspiracy but the patent record confirms. Numerous patents related to alternative energy have been classified under national security provisions, removed from public access not because they are dangerous but because they threaten the economic architecture built on scarcity. The broader pattern was established a century ago by the Phoebus cartel, a documented conspiracy from 1924 to 1939 in which the world's major lightbulb manufacturers agreed to deliberately shorten bulb lifespan from 2,500 hours to 1,000 hours, the first proven case of industrial planned obsolescence. This philosophy, building products to fail so they must be replaced, has since been embedded into every consumer industry, ensuring perpetual consumption and waste. The struggle between Nikola Tesla's vision of freely transmitted energy and Thomas Edison's metered delivery model was not merely a technical dispute. It was a civilizational choice between energy as a commons and energy as a commodity. The commodity model won, and the architecture of metering, billing, and artificial scarcity has defined the energy system ever since.

Meanwhile, 759 million people on Earth live without access to electricity, a condition of energy poverty that exists not because the technology to serve them is unavailable but because serving them is not profitable. Nuclear testing, conducted over 2,000 times across the globe, was disproportionately carried out on indigenous lands, from the Marshall Islands to the Nevada Test Site to French Polynesia, treating indigenous populations and their territories as expendable sacrifice zones. The Hubbert curve and peak oil narratives have been deployed to justify price increases and resource wars while the actual dynamics of energy supply remain obscured behind industry-controlled data. The energy monopoly is not merely an economic arrangement. It is the foundation upon which the entire architecture of control is built, because whoever controls how a civilization is powered controls the civilization itself.
