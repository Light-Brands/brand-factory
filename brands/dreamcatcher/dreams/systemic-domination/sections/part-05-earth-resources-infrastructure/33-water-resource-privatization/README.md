# Water & Natural Resource Privatization -- Enclosing the Commons

_Part of the [Systemic Domination](../../../README.md) thesis._

## Content

### Opening Hook

In April 2000, the Bolivian city of Cochabamba erupted. The government, under pressure from the World Bank, had privatized its municipal water system, awarding a forty-year concession to Aguas del Tunari, a consortium controlled by the Bechtel Corporation of San Francisco. Within weeks, water rates rose by as much as 300 percent. Families earning less than sixty dollars a month were suddenly paying twenty dollars or more for water -- a quarter of their income for the substance without which life ceases in three days. The company had been granted rights not merely to the municipal system but to all water in the region, including rainwater. Under the terms of the concession, it became technically illegal for residents to collect rain falling on their own roofs. When the people of Cochabamba took to the streets, the government responded with tear gas, rubber bullets, and eventually live ammunition. A seventeen-year-old boy, Victor Hugo Daza, was shot through the face by a military sniper. The revolt did not stop. After months of general strikes, highway blockades, and sustained civil disobedience, the Cochabamba Water War succeeded: the contract was rescinded, the company expelled, and water was returned to public control. It remains one of the only successful reversals of resource privatization in modern history. Bechtel, from its offices in San Francisco, sued Bolivia for $25 million in lost profits.

Meanwhile, on the other side of the hemisphere, a different water story was unfolding with less dramatic resistance and more devastating consequences. In 2014, the city of Flint, Michigan -- a majority-Black city hollowed out by deindustrialization -- switched its water source from the Detroit system to the Flint River as a cost-cutting measure. State-appointed emergency managers, who had replaced the elected local government, approved the switch without implementing corrosion control treatment. Lead leached from aging pipes into the drinking water at levels up to 13,200 parts per billion -- nearly nine hundred times the EPA action level of 15 ppb. For eighteen months, officials at every level dismissed residents' complaints, suppressed testing data, and insisted the water was safe. Children were poisoned. Legionnaires' disease outbreaks killed at least twelve people. Throughout the crisis, Flint residents paid among the highest water rates in the United States -- for water they could not safely drink. Ninety miles away, the Nestle corporation (now BlueTriton Brands) was extracting up to 400 gallons per minute from Michigan aquifers, paying the state $200 per year in permitting fees while selling the water back to consumers at a markup of several thousand percent. The juxtaposition is not ironic. It is architectural.

### The Architecture

The privatization of water and natural resources is not a recent phenomenon but the latest iteration of a pattern stretching back centuries: the enclosure of the commons. The English Enclosure Acts, enacted in successive waves from 1604 to 1914, systematically dismantled the common-field system that had sustained English peasant communities for generations. Land that had been collectively managed -- for grazing, foraging, fuel gathering, and subsistence farming -- was fenced, surveyed, and transferred to private ownership. An estimated 6.8 million acres of common land were enclosed during this period, displacing millions of rural people and forcing them into urban factories where they became the cheap labor supply upon which industrial capitalism was built. The poet John Clare, witnessing the destruction of his Northamptonshire commons in the 1820s, described enclosure as the theft of the landscape itself: "Inclosure came and trampled on the grave / Of Labour's rights and left the poor a slave." What happened to English commons in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries is now happening to every remaining commons on Earth -- water, seeds, minerals, genetic sequences, the electromagnetic spectrum, the ocean floor, the orbit of the planet, and the atmosphere itself.

The modern architecture of water privatization operates through several institutional channels. The World Bank and the International Monetary Fund have been the most consequential accelerants. Throughout the 1990s and 2000s, the World Bank made water privatization an explicit condition of development loans in dozens of countries. A 2000 internal policy paper stated that the Bank would support privatization of water utilities "wherever possible." This was not neutral economic advice. It was the leveraging of financial desperation to force open public systems for corporate extraction. Countries in debt crisis -- themselves often indebted through structural adjustment programs imposed by the same institutions -- were told that if they wanted relief, they would open their water to the market. The corporations waiting on the other side were a small cartel: Veolia (formerly Vivendi), Suez (now part of Veolia), and Thames Water dominated the global water privatization market, operating in over 100 countries. The results were consistent: rates increased, service to unprofitable (poor) communities deteriorated, infrastructure investment declined, and profits were extracted to corporate headquarters in Paris and London while the pipes beneath Buenos Aires, Manila, and Jakarta continued to corrode.

The resource enclosure extends far beyond water. Mineral rights in the United States illustrate the architecture with brutal clarity. In many states, mineral rights are legally severable from surface rights -- meaning the minerals beneath your property can be owned by someone else entirely, and that owner can access those minerals regardless of what happens to the surface. In practice, this means a fracking company can drill under your home, inject millions of gallons of chemically treated water into the rock beneath your children's bedrooms, and you have no legal recourse. The 2005 Energy Policy Act -- the so-called "Halliburton Loophole," named for the company whose former CEO, Dick Cheney, was serving as Vice President when it was passed -- explicitly exempted hydraulic fracturing from the Safe Drinking Water Act, the Clean Air Act, and the Clean Water Act. Fracking operations have since contaminated groundwater in communities across Pennsylvania, West Virginia, Colorado, Texas, and North Dakota. Residents in Dimock, Pennsylvania, could light their tap water on fire due to methane contamination. The industry's response, consistent and coordinated, has been to deny causation, fund counter-research, and lobby state legislatures for laws that prevent disclosure of fracking chemicals as "trade secrets."

Seed enclosure represents perhaps the most existentially threatening form of commons privatization. Three corporations -- Bayer-Monsanto, Corteva (formerly Dow-DuPont), and Syngenta-ChemChina -- control approximately 53 percent of the global commercial seed market. Through patents on genetically modified organisms and increasingly on conventionally bred seed varieties, these companies have converted the basis of all terrestrial food production into proprietary intellectual property. Monsanto (before its acquisition by Bayer) sued over 400 farmers for "patent infringement," including cases where patented genetic material had blown or cross-pollinated onto their fields without their knowledge or consent. Percy Schmeiser, a Canadian canola farmer whose fields were contaminated by neighboring Roundup Ready crops, was sued by Monsanto and taken to the Supreme Court of Canada. The court ruled in Monsanto's favor. The implications are civilizational: the seed -- humanity's oldest technology, refined through ten thousand years of collective cultivation, saved and shared as a commons across every culture on Earth -- is being enclosed within the patent portfolios of three multinational corporations. Farmers who save seeds from their own harvest now risk prosecution. The ancient practice that sustained every civilization in human history has been made illegal.

The final frontier of enclosure is the global commons: the oceans, the atmosphere, and outer space. Fishing rights, once held in common, are increasingly privatized through Individual Transferable Quotas, or "catch shares," which consolidate fishing access into the hands of a few large operators while destroying small-scale fishing communities. Carbon credit markets, established under the Kyoto Protocol and expanded through subsequent frameworks, transform the atmosphere into a tradeable commodity -- the right to pollute becomes an asset to be bought and sold, with wealthy nations and corporations purchasing atmospheric capacity from poorer ones. Orbital space is being enclosed by satellite mega-constellations: SpaceX's Starlink alone has launched over 5,000 satellites, occupying orbital lanes that effectively exclude competitors and creating debris risks that may eventually make certain orbital altitudes unusable. The deep ocean floor, containing polymetallic nodules, rare earth minerals, and hydrothermal vent ecosystems that scientists have barely begun to study, is being divided into mining concessions by the International Seabed Authority. In every case, the pattern is identical: a resource held in common is declared a market, access is auctioned, the wealthy acquire rights, and the rest of humanity -- including the unborn -- loses what was never anyone's to sell.

### Key Mechanisms of Control

- **Conditional Lending for Privatization** -- The World Bank and IMF made water privatization an explicit condition of development loans throughout the 1990s and 2000s, leveraging debt crises to force open public systems for corporate extraction. Countries in financial distress had no meaningful choice: accept privatization or lose access to international credit.

- **Severable Mineral Rights** -- Legal frameworks that separate surface rights from subsurface mineral rights allow extraction companies to operate beneath private property without the owner's consent. The Halliburton Loophole (2005) exempted fracking from major environmental regulations, removing the last legal barriers to uncontrolled subsurface extraction.

- **Seed Patents and Genetic Enclosure** -- Three corporations control over half the global commercial seed market. Patent law applied to living organisms converts ten thousand years of collective agricultural knowledge into corporate intellectual property. Seed saving, the most ancient of human practices, is criminalized under patent enforcement.

- **Water Rate Exploitation** -- Following privatization, water rates consistently increase while service to low-income communities deteriorates. Flint residents paid among the highest rates in America for lead-contaminated water while corporations extracted nearby aquifer water for $200 per year.

- **Infrastructure Neglect as Displacement** -- The deliberate failure to maintain public water infrastructure in low-income and minority communities functions as a slow-motion forced migration, driving residents out of municipalities that can then be redeveloped for higher-income populations.

- **Carbon and Atmospheric Markets** -- Cap-and-trade systems and carbon credit markets transform the atmosphere into a tradeable commodity, allowing wealthy polluters to purchase the right to continue emitting while the costs of climate change are borne by those who can afford neither credits nor adaptation.

- **Catch Shares and Fishing Enclosure** -- Individual Transferable Quotas consolidate fishing rights into a small number of large operators, destroying small-scale fishing communities that sustained coastal populations for centuries and concentrating food supply control.

- **Trade Secret Protections for Toxins** -- Fracking companies in multiple states are legally permitted to withhold the chemical composition of injection fluids as proprietary "trade secrets," preventing communities from knowing what is being pumped into the ground beneath their homes.

- **Orbital and Deep-Sea Enclosure** -- Satellite mega-constellations occupy orbital lanes as de facto private property. Deep-sea mining concessions parcel the ocean floor before science has documented its ecosystems. The last commons on Earth are being enclosed before most people realize they exist.

- **Legal Suppression of Resistance** -- Environmental defenders who resist resource privatization face criminalization, violence, and assassination. Global Witness documented over 1,700 environmental defenders killed between 2012 and 2021, the vast majority in communities resisting resource extraction in the Global South.

### Independent Conditioning

Water privatization and resource enclosure condition human consciousness even in the absence of every other system described in this thesis. The most fundamental conditioning is the normalization of the idea that life's essentials can be owned. When water -- the most basic prerequisite for biological existence, a substance that falls from the sky, that moves through cycles older than terrestrial life itself -- is successfully reframed as a commodity, an extraordinary psychological threshold has been crossed. If water can be owned, anything can be owned. The enclosure of water establishes the conceptual framework within which all other enclosures become possible. It teaches populations, from earliest childhood, that access to what sustains life is not a right but a market transaction, and that those who cannot pay are not victims of injustice but simply unsuccessful market participants.

This conditioning produces a deep, largely unconscious fatalism. When people accept that water is a commodity, they have already accepted the foundational premise of the entire domination architecture: that the gifts of the living world are inventory to be priced, that human need is a market opportunity, and that the question "Who owns the rain?" has a legitimate answer other than "No one." The psychological weight of this acceptance is difficult to overstate. It forecloses the conceptual space within which alternatives might be imagined. If the commons have always been private -- if that is simply "how the world works" -- then challenging privatization feels not like political action but like denying reality. The system does not need to defend the enclosure of the commons through argument. It needs only to make enclosure seem natural, inevitable, and eternal. Once that perception is established, resistance requires not merely political will but an act of imagination: the ability to conceive of a world organized on fundamentally different principles.

The conditioning also operates through fear. In communities where water has been privatized and rates have increased beyond affordability, the daily anxiety of water insecurity reshapes consciousness in the same way that food insecurity and housing insecurity do. Parents who cannot guarantee clean water for their children experience chronic stress that degrades health, shortens time horizons, and collapses cognitive bandwidth toward immediate survival. This stress is not a side effect of privatization; it is its disciplinary function. Populations anxious about water do not organize against the system that made them anxious. They scramble to pay the bill.

### Interlocking with Other Systems

The privatization of water and natural resources does not operate in isolation. It is embedded within and reinforced by a network of interlocking systems that collectively ensure enclosure is maintained, expanded, and treated as natural law.

The Financial System & Capitalism (Section 23) provides the core logic: commodification. Under the financial architecture, value is recognized only when something is priced and traded. Water in a river has no economic value; water in a bottle, a futures contract, or a privatized utility has immense value. In December 2020, water futures began trading on the Chicago Mercantile Exchange for the first time, completing a transformation that Cochabamba's protesters had warned about two decades earlier. The financial system's fundamental requirement -- that everything must generate returns for capital -- is what drives the enclosure of every commons. Ecocide & Planetary Grief (Section 32) is both cause and consequence: the contamination of surface water through industrial agriculture, fracking, and chemical dumping makes public water supplies unreliable, creating the very crisis that privatization claims to solve. Polluted water becomes the justification for corporate water systems, which then charge premium prices for what was freely available before industry poisoned it. The fox poisons the water and then sells the filter.

Colonialism & Neo-Colonialism (Section 43) provides the geographic template. The nations most aggressively targeted for water privatization -- Bolivia, Ghana, Tanzania, the Philippines, Argentina -- are overwhelmingly Global South countries whose debt crises were themselves products of colonial and neo-colonial extraction. The IMF's structural adjustment conditions replicate the logic of colonial resource seizure: take their wealth, indebt them, then force them to sell their remaining commons to repay the debt. The Food Supply (Section 08) is directly threatened by seed enclosure. When three corporations control over half the global seed market and patent law criminalizes seed saving, the ability of nations and communities to feed themselves is held hostage by corporate intellectual property portfolios. The Energy Monopoly (Section 34) connects through fossil fuel water contamination: fracking operations consume between 1.5 and 16 million gallons of water per well, and the wastewater -- laden with heavy metals, radioactive materials, and undisclosed chemicals -- contaminates aquifers that communities depend on for drinking water and agriculture.

Housing & Shelter (Section 27) intersects through the hidden cost of water: in privatized systems, water and sewer rates become a significant and growing component of housing costs, particularly for low-income households who spend a disproportionate share of income on utilities. The Education System (Section 14) contributes through absence -- there is no education in the commons, no curriculum that teaches children what a commons is, how enclosure works historically, or what rights they hold to the shared inheritance of the living world. This ignorance is prerequisite to enclosure: you cannot defend what you have never been taught exists. The Media (Section 15) suppresses coverage of water crises in favor of narratives that treat privatization as progress and public systems as inefficient, while providing virtually no investigative coverage of the corporate actors driving enclosure or the communities resisting it. The I vs We Mentality (Section 54) provides the ideological foundation: the framing of resource access as an individual market transaction rather than a collective right is itself a product of the atomized individualism that pervades Western consciousness. When the self is understood as a competitive unit rather than a member of a commons, enclosure feels logical rather than criminal. Modern Government (Section 38) enables enclosure through regulatory frameworks that favor corporate access to resources, through the permitting systems that charge corporations nominal fees for massive extraction, and through the emergency management structures -- as in Flint -- that override democratic governance in favor of austerity-driven decisions that harm the most vulnerable. The Architecture of Liberation (Section 58) reveals the counter-pattern: water cooperatives, community land trusts, seed libraries, indigenous water governance systems, and the global commons movement represent living alternatives to enclosure that demonstrate, in practice, that another relationship to the shared inheritance of the earth is possible.

### Sacred Laws Violated

The enclosure of the commons violates the most fundamental principles of sacred law, because it attacks the relationship between life and the conditions that sustain life.

The **Law of Abundance** is the most directly violated. Water is the most abundant compound on the surface of the earth. It covers seventy-one percent of the planet. It falls from the sky. It circulates through a hydrological cycle that has operated for billions of years without interruption, purifying and redistributing itself without human intervention. The idea that this substance can be scarce -- that access to it must be rationed through market mechanisms controlled by private corporations -- is a manufactured lie of extraordinary audacity. Scarcity of water is not a natural condition; it is an engineered condition, produced by contamination (which degrades supply), by infrastructure neglect (which prevents delivery), and by legal enclosure (which restricts access). The same logic applies to seeds, which reproduce themselves by nature, to soil, which regenerates through biological cycles, and to sunlight, which arrives each morning without invoicing. The commons are abundant by design. Scarcity is imposed by enclosure. The **Law of Stewardship** is violated when resources that belong to the community of life are claimed as private property. Stewardship implies a relationship of care and custodianship -- the understanding that water, land, seeds, and minerals are not possessions but trusts, held for the benefit of all life, present and future. When a corporation claims ownership of an aquifer, it is not merely making an economic transaction; it is violating a sacred covenant between the living and the living world. The **Law of Right Relationship** is violated whenever the balance between taking and returning is destroyed. Extraction without reciprocity -- drawing down aquifers without recharge, depleting soil without restoration, harvesting fish beyond regenerative capacity -- is the definition of right relationship violated. The enclosure of the commons converts a relationship of reciprocity into a relationship of parasitism, taking everything and returning nothing but waste, debt, and depleted systems that future generations will inherit without remedy.

## Open Questions

- If water is now traded as a futures commodity on the Chicago Mercantile Exchange, what prevents the same speculative dynamics that destabilized food prices in 2007-2008 from being applied to water, and what would a water price crisis look like for the poorest communities?
- The Cochabamba Water War remains one of the only successful reversals of privatization -- what specific conditions enabled that reversal, and can they be replicated in communities without the same tradition of organized resistance?
- How do we legally and constitutionally redefine the commons in a system where property law is foundational -- is the "rights of nature" framework (Ecuador, New Zealand, Colombia) scalable, or does effective commons protection require an entirely different legal architecture?
- What role do indigenous water governance systems -- which managed shared resources for millennia without privatization -- play in designing post-enclosure alternatives, and how do we engage those systems without reproducing extractive dynamics?
- If seed saving has been criminalized through patent enforcement, what is the tipping point at which corporate seed control becomes a food security emergency, and which nations are most vulnerable?
