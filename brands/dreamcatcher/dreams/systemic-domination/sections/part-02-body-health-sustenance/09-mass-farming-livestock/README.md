## Content

Industrial animal agriculture represents one of the starkest examples of a system in which the relentless pursuit of scale and profit has produced conditions so grotesque that the industry spends enormous resources ensuring the public never sees them. Factory farms, which now produce over 99% of farmed animals in the United States, confine billions of living creatures in conditions that would constitute criminal cruelty if applied to a single dog or cat: battery cages so small hens cannot spread their wings, gestation crates that immobilize breeding sows for the duration of their pregnancies, feedlots where cattle stand in their own waste while being pumped with antibiotics to survive conditions that would otherwise kill them. The scale is staggering. Over 80 billion land animals are slaughtered for food globally each year. The industry has fought relentlessly for "ag-gag" laws that criminalize the act of documenting conditions inside these facilities, a legal strategy that reveals a simple truth: the system cannot survive public awareness of what it actually does.

The health consequences extend far beyond the animals themselves. The routine use of antibiotics in livestock, accounting for approximately 70% of all medically important antibiotics sold in the United States, is accelerating the development of antibiotic-resistant bacteria, a crisis the World Health Organization has identified as one of the greatest threats to global health. The conditions of industrial farming create ideal breeding grounds for zoonotic disease: H5N1 avian flu, swine flu (H1N1), and numerous other pathogens have emerged directly from the density and squalor of factory farm operations. The processed meat that emerges from these systems is classified by the WHO as a Group 1 carcinogen. The workers inside slaughterhouses, disproportionately immigrants and people of color, suffer injury rates far exceeding the national average and are subjected to line speeds so fast that repetitive stress injuries and lacerations are endemic. The psychological toll on slaughterhouse workers, including elevated rates of PTSD, substance abuse, and domestic violence, is well documented but rarely discussed, because acknowledging it would require acknowledging what the work actually involves.

The environmental toll is equally devastating. Animal agriculture is responsible for an estimated 14.5% of global greenhouse gas emissions, more than the entire transportation sector. It is the leading driver of deforestation, particularly in the Amazon, where rainforest is cleared for cattle ranching and soybean production to feed livestock. It consumes approximately one-third of the planet's ice-free land and is the primary cause of species extinction through habitat destruction. The system uses roughly 70% of global agricultural land to produce food that delivers only 18% of global calories, an inefficiency so profound it can only be sustained through massive subsidies and the externalization of environmental costs onto the public. The entire architecture of industrial animal agriculture is a system that treats living beings as production units, workers as expendable, consumers as ignorant, and the planet as an externality, all in the service of producing the cheapest possible protein for a market that has been conditioned to never ask what cheapest actually costs.
