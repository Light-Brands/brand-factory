# Cheap Quality Over Craftsmanship -- Destroying the Art of Making Things Well

_Part of the [Systemic Domination](../../../README.md) thesis._

---

## Content

### Opening Hook

On December 23, 1924, representatives from the world's leading lightbulb manufacturers -- Osram, Philips, General Electric's international subsidiary, Compagnie des Lampes, and others -- gathered in Geneva to sign what would become known as the Phoebus cartel agreement. At the time, commercially available incandescent bulbs routinely lasted 2,500 hours or more. Some designs approached 3,000. The cartel's explicit, documented purpose was to reduce the standard lifespan to 1,000 hours. Member companies that produced bulbs exceeding the target were fined. Internal testing laboratories were established to ensure compliance with the degradation mandate. The engineering challenge was not how to make a better lightbulb. It was how to make a worse one -- reliably, precisely, and profitably. The Phoebus cartel did not invent the lightbulb. It invented the lightbulb that dies on schedule. And in doing so, it formalized a principle that would come to govern virtually every manufactured object on Earth: planned obsolescence, the deliberate engineering of failure into products to ensure that consumers must buy again and again and again.

Nearly a century later, the principle had become so pervasive that it barely registered as an act of engineering at all. In December 2017, Apple confirmed what independent researchers and millions of frustrated users had long suspected: the company had been deliberately throttling the performance of older iPhones through software updates, a practice that came to be known as "Batterygate." Apple claimed the throttling was intended to prevent unexpected shutdowns caused by aging batteries. Critics noted that the effect was indistinguishable from the effect of planned obsolescence -- slower phones drove upgrade purchases, and Apple had disclosed neither the throttling nor the simple alternative of a $29 battery replacement. France's consumer fraud agency, the DGCCRF, fined Apple 25 million euros. Italy's antitrust authority fined the company an additional 10 million euros. A class action settlement in the United States cost Apple $113 million. These were rounding errors on a company whose annual iPhone revenue exceeded $190 billion. The fines were not deterrents. They were operating costs -- the price of doing business in an economy where the engineered death of products is the engineered life of profits.

### The Architecture

The system of cheap quality over craftsmanship operates through an interlocking set of economic, legal, cultural, and educational mechanisms that collectively ensure products are designed to fail, skills are designed to disappear, and consumers are designed to accept both as natural. It is not a single conspiracy but a convergence of incentives so powerful and so aligned that they produce the same outcome across every industry, every nation, and every product category: the systematic replacement of durability with disposability, of skill with speed, and of care with cost.

The first structural pillar is the quarterly earnings cycle. Publicly traded corporations are legally and culturally compelled to demonstrate growth every three months. This is not a suggestion; it is the gravitational force that shapes every decision from the boardroom to the factory floor. A product that lasts twenty years generates one sale per customer per generation. A product that fails in two years generates ten. Under the logic of quarterly earnings, the durable product is the inferior business model regardless of its superior quality, and the executive who proposes building things to last is proposing, in Wall Street's terms, a smaller addressable market. The financialization of manufacturing -- the subordination of engineering decisions to shareholder expectations -- has transformed the question "How well can we make this?" into "How cheaply can we make this while maintaining the perception of adequate quality for the minimum expected lifespan?" These are profoundly different questions, and the second one has been winning for decades.

The second pillar is the destruction of repair infrastructure. A product that can be repaired is a product that does not need to be replaced, which makes repair the natural enemy of the disposability model. Accordingly, the past forty years have seen a systematic assault on the ability to fix things. Manufacturers use proprietary screws, glued-in batteries, serialized components that refuse to function when transplanted, and software locks that brick devices repaired by unauthorized technicians. John Deere's tractors -- machines that farmers depend upon for their livelihoods -- require proprietary diagnostic software that only authorized dealers can access, turning a $400,000 piece of equipment into a subscription service that the nominal owner does not fully control. Apple's longstanding refusal to sell parts or schematics to independent repair shops forced customers to use Apple's own repair services at Apple's own prices, or to replace the device entirely. The right-to-repair movement, which has gained legislative traction in the European Union and several U.S. states, exists precisely because manufacturers have been so successful at engineering repairability out of their products. They did not merely stop making things that last. They made it illegal, or practically impossible, to keep things alive.

The third pillar is the de-skilling of the workforce. In a craft economy, the maker's skill is the primary source of value. A skilled carpenter, welder, cobbler, or tailor produces objects of enduring quality because the knowledge in their hands, accumulated over years of apprenticeship and practice, is irreplaceable. The industrial model inverts this relationship. Value is created not by the skill of the worker but by the speed of the assembly line, the cheapness of the materials, and the volume of units shipped. The worker becomes a replaceable input -- interchangeable, unskilled, and therefore cheap. This is not an accident of industrialization; it is its purpose. Frederick Winslow Taylor's "scientific management" of the early 1900s explicitly sought to transfer knowledge from workers to managers, breaking complex tasks into simple repetitive motions that any untrained person could perform. Henry Ford's assembly line perfected the model. The result, a century later, is a global manufacturing system in which the vast majority of workers have no relationship with the finished product, no understanding of its design, and no investment in its quality. They are hands, not craftspeople. And when those hands can be replaced by robots -- as they increasingly are -- the de-skilling is complete.

The fourth pillar is the cultural conditioning of the consumer. A population that values craftsmanship would not tolerate planned obsolescence. The system therefore requires consumers trained to equate value with price, who experience replacing a broken object as normal rather than outrageous, and who have never developed the tactile literacy to distinguish between something well-made and something cheaply produced. This conditioning begins in childhood, with toys designed to break within weeks, and continues through adulthood, with marketing that frames newness as inherently desirable and repaired objects as inferior. The language itself has been colonized: "affordable" is a euphemism for cheap; "upgrade" implies improvement when it often means replacing a functioning product with a marginally different one; "vintage" and "artisan" have been appropriated by mass-market brands to sell factory products at handmade prices. The consumer who cannot tell the difference between solid wood and particleboard veneer, between full-grain leather and bonded leather dust, is not stupid. They were never taught.

The fifth pillar is the defunding of craft education. Shop classes, home economics, vocational training, and apprenticeship programs have been systematically eliminated from public schools across the developed world, replaced by curricula oriented toward standardized testing and college preparation. In the United States, enrollment in vocational education courses dropped 14 percent between 1990 and 2009, and many school districts eliminated woodworking, metalworking, and textile programs entirely. The message: working with your hands is lower-status than working with your mind, and the path to success runs through a university degree, not a workshop. The result is a generation that cannot fix a leaky faucet, sharpen a knife, or assess the structural integrity of a piece of furniture -- dependent on the marketplace for every material need. The elimination of craft education is not a budget decision. It is an infrastructure decision -- the deliberate removal of the knowledge base that would allow people to opt out of the disposability economy.

### Key Mechanisms of Control

- **Planned Obsolescence** -- The deliberate engineering of product failure on a predetermined timeline. Originated formally with the Phoebus cartel (1924) for lightbulbs, perfected by the postwar consumer economy, and now embedded in virtually every product category from smartphones to washing machines. Products are designed with non-replaceable batteries, breakable plastic gears where metal ones once stood, firmware updates that slow older devices, and fashion cycles that render functional products "outdated." The engineering departments at major manufacturers employ teams specifically tasked with lifecycle management -- a euphemism for determining exactly when a product should stop working.

- **Anti-Repair Design** -- The systematic engineering of products to resist repair: proprietary fasteners, glued-in components, serialized parts that reject third-party replacements, software locks that brick devices serviced outside authorized channels, and void-on-opening warranty stickers. John Deere's tractor software locks, Apple's parts-pairing restrictions, and the auto industry's resistance to OBD (on-board diagnostics) access all serve the same function: ensuring that when a product breaks, replacement is easier and often cheaper than repair.

- **Right-to-Repair Suppression** -- Active corporate lobbying against legislation that would require manufacturers to provide parts, tools, and repair manuals to independent shops and consumers. The technology and automotive industries have spent millions opposing right-to-repair bills in state legislatures across the United States. When legislation does pass, manufacturers find creative methods of circumvention -- Apple's self-service repair program, for instance, requires users to rent a 79-pound toolkit and follow procedures so complex that most consumers abandon the attempt.

- **De-Skilling of Labor** -- The Taylorist fragmentation of complex craft work into simple repetitive tasks that require no training and therefore command no premium. The assembly line eliminated the craftsperson; automation eliminates even the assembly worker. Each generation possesses less material knowledge than the one before, and each generation is correspondingly more dependent on the marketplace for goods that their grandparents could have made or repaired themselves.

- **Defunded Craft Education** -- The removal of shop classes, home economics, vocational training, and apprenticeship pathways from public education, replaced by test-focused curricula that prioritize abstract knowledge over embodied skill. Germany's dual education system, which maintains robust apprenticeship pathways, stands as an exception -- and not coincidentally, Germany remains a global leader in precision manufacturing and engineering quality.

- **Fast Fashion Cycle** -- The compression of clothing production cycles from four seasonal collections per year to 52 or more "micro-seasons," with brands like Shein releasing thousands of new designs daily. This acceleration makes durability irrelevant: garments are designed to be worn a handful of times, photographed for social media, and discarded. The average garment is now worn only seven times before disposal. Quality construction is an obstacle to this model, not an asset.

- **Ikea-fication of the Home** -- The normalization of flat-pack, particle-board, Allen-key-assembled furniture designed with a lifespan measured in moves rather than decades. IKEA sells approximately 100 million pieces of furniture annually, most of it engineered for a single assembly. The company's own internal research has acknowledged that most of its products cannot survive a second disassembly and reassembly. The result is a global furniture culture in which "buying new" is cheaper and easier than moving or repairing what you own -- and where the concept of an heirloom piece, furniture meant to survive a family's lifetime, has been reduced to a luxury niche.

- **Disposable Electronics and E-Waste** -- The average smartphone is replaced every 2.5 years; the average laptop, every 3-5 years. Over 50 million metric tons of e-waste are generated globally each year, making it the fastest-growing waste stream on Earth. Only 17% is formally recycled. The remainder is landfilled or shipped to informal processing operations in the Global South, where workers -- frequently children -- disassemble devices by hand, exposed to lead, mercury, cadmium, and brominated flame retardants. The devices are designed to be difficult to repair and impossible to upgrade, ensuring maximum throughput from consumer to landfill.

- **Material Substitution** -- The systematic replacement of durable natural materials with cheaper synthetic alternatives that mimic the appearance of quality while delivering a fraction of the lifespan. Solid wood replaced by MDF and particleboard. Leather replaced by bonded leather dust or polyurethane. Metal hardware replaced by plastic. Glass replaced by polycarbonate. Cotton replaced by polyester blends. Each substitution reduces cost, reduces durability, and reduces the consumer's ability to assess what they are actually buying.

- **Warranty Limitation and Lemon Laws Circumvention** -- Warranties have shortened dramatically across product categories, normalizing the expectation that products will fail within their designed lifespan. The legal infrastructure of consumer protection has been steadily weakened through mandatory arbitration clauses, class-action waivers, and the replacement of meaningful warranties with upsold "extended protection plans" that generate additional revenue from the fear of the very failure the manufacturer engineered.

- **Marketing of Newness as Progress** -- The cultural apparatus that frames each product generation as a meaningful improvement over the last, even when the changes are cosmetic, trivial, or actively harmful to longevity. Annual product launches, version numbering systems, and "innovation" marketing create a perpetual treadmill in which owning last year's model becomes a social liability. The psychological mechanism is the same one that drives fast fashion: not need, but manufactured desire.

### Independent Conditioning

Even in the absence of every other system described in this thesis, the destruction of craftsmanship would fundamentally alter human consciousness. The act of making something well -- of selecting materials with care, of developing skill through years of practice, of producing an object that embodies the maker's knowledge, attention, and intention -- is one of the oldest and most profound expressions of human dignity. It connects the maker to the material world in a relationship of intimacy and respect. It requires patience, the willingness to fail and learn, the capacity to hold a standard of excellence that transcends market incentives. When a society systematically eliminates the conditions that make this kind of making possible -- by defunding education, devaluing trade skills, engineering products for disposability, and training consumers to equate cheapness with value -- it does not merely change its economy. It changes its soul.

The disposable mindset extends far beyond objects. A civilization trained to treat everything as replaceable develops a corresponding relationship with the non-material world. If a shirt is disposable, why not a friendship? If furniture is not worth repairing, why invest in fixing a relationship? If tools are meant to be thrown away, what does that say about workers, about communities, about commitments? The logic of planned obsolescence, internalized across a lifetime of consumer training, becomes a general orientation toward existence: nothing lasts, nothing is worth maintaining, nothing carries enough meaning to justify the effort of preservation. This is not a metaphor. Studies on consumer behavior have documented that people who habitually discard objects are less likely to invest in long-term relationships, less likely to maintain community ties, and more likely to experience the anomie and rootlessness that characterize late-capitalist social life. The throwaway economy produces throwaway people -- not because anyone intended that outcome, but because the logic of disposability, once learned, does not confine itself to material goods.

The loss of craft knowledge represents a particularly devastating form of cultural amnesia. When the last cobbler closes his shop, when the last furniture maker sells her tools, when the last weaver forgets the patterns her grandmother taught her, knowledge that took centuries to accumulate vanishes in a single generation. This is not the kind of knowledge that can be reconstructed from textbooks or YouTube tutorials. It is embodied knowledge -- the sensitivity in the fingertips that can feel when a joint is true, the eye that can read wood grain and anticipate how a board will move as it dries, the muscle memory that guides a chisel through a mortise. This knowledge lives in the hands and dies with the hands that hold it. A civilization that permits this loss -- that does not merely permit it but engineers it through economic incentives that make craft unviable -- has severed itself from one of the deepest sources of meaning, competence, and self-reliance that human beings have ever known.

### Interlocking with Other Systems

The destruction of craftsmanship does not operate in isolation. It is embedded in a network of mutually reinforcing systems that make planned obsolescence both profitable and invisible, that ensure consumers lack the knowledge to resist it and the infrastructure to escape it.

The Financial System & Capitalism (Section 23) provides the gravitational force: quarterly earnings cycles demand perpetual revenue growth, and a product that lasts a lifetime is, by Wall Street's metrics, a failed business model. Cost externalization ensures that the true price of a disposable product never appears on its price tag. Ecocide (Section 32) is the physical consequence: 92 million tons of textile waste annually, 50 million tons of e-waste, landfills expanding across rural landscapes, microplastics saturating every ocean basin. Every product engineered to fail is engineered to become waste, and the waste does not disappear. It accumulates.

Labor & Work Culture (Section 26) provides the human substrate. The Taylorist revolution did not improve efficiency; it transferred power from workers to managers by fragmenting craft knowledge into meaningless fragments. A skilled tradesperson possessed autonomy, expertise, and community standing. An assembly-line worker possesses none. Fake Materials & Synthetic Clothing (Section 31) operates as the material companion: petroleum-derived synthetics replacing natural materials is the cheapening of inputs that makes disposability profitable. Polyester replaces cotton. Particleboard replaces hardwood. Vinyl replaces leather. Each substitution accelerates the journey from factory to landfill.

Colonialism & Neo-Colonialism (Section 43) provides the geography. Cheap products are cheap because they are manufactured in the Global South under conditions Western consumers would not tolerate: garment factories in Bangladesh, electronics assembly in China, rare-earth mining in the Congo. The colonial template of peripheral extraction for core consumption is replicated in every supply chain. The Education System (Section 14) ensures the knowledge needed to resist is never transmitted: shop classes eliminated, vocational training stigmatized, home economics abandoned. The Energy Monopoly (Section 34) fuels the machine: planned obsolescence drives perpetual extraction, manufacturing, and global transportation, all powered by fossil fuels whose costs are externalized. The "I vs We" Mentality (Section 54) provides the psychological infrastructure: radical individualism frames purchasing as personal choice rather than systemic outcome, atomizing resistance into consumer guilt. Architecture of Liberation (Section 58) represents the counter-movement: repair cafes, maker spaces, right-to-repair legislation, and apprenticeship revival constitute direct challenges to planned obsolescence.

### Sacred Laws Violated

The destruction of craftsmanship violates sacred principles at the deepest level, because it attacks the human relationship with creation itself -- the act of making, the ethic of care, and the respect for materials that connects the maker to the living world.

The **Law of Creative Expression** -- the principle that human beings are inherently creative and that the expression of this creativity through making, building, and shaping is a fundamental aspect of spiritual wholeness -- is systematically suppressed when the conditions for meaningful creation are eliminated. A society that de-skills its workers, defunds its craft education, and engineers its products for disposability has declared that making things well is not a valued human activity. It has replaced the craftsperson's relationship with materials -- a relationship of knowledge, respect, and reciprocal engagement -- with the factory's relationship: extraction, manipulation, and discard. The **Law of Reverence for Life** is violated every time a product is designed to become waste, because that design decision treats the natural materials from which the product was made -- the trees felled for particleboard, the petroleum extracted for plastics, the metals mined for electronics -- as worthless beyond their momentary utility. A culture of disposability is a culture that cannot reverence the material world, because it has been trained to see materials as inputs rather than as gifts from living systems that deserve care in their use and return. The **Law of Reciprocity** -- the principle that what is taken must be returned, that extraction must be balanced by restoration -- is obliterated by the linear metabolism of the disposability economy: take, make, use, discard. Nothing is returned. Nothing is restored. The waste stream flows in one direction only, from the living world to the landfill, and the cycle of reciprocity that governed human material culture for millennia has been broken. The **Law of Natural Rhythm** is violated by the artificial acceleration of consumption cycles. Nature operates in long rhythms: a tree grows for decades before it is ready to become timber; wool is shorn once a year; flax grows through a full season before it becomes linen. The disposability economy overrides these rhythms with quarterly earnings reports and weekly product drops, imposing an industrial tempo on materials and makers alike. The result is not efficiency but exhaustion -- of resources, of workers, of the land, and of the human capacity to find meaning in the things we make and use.

## Evidence & Data

- **The Phoebus cartel (1924) formally agreed to reduce lightbulb lifespan from 2,500 to 1,000 hours; member companies were fined for exceeding the target** (Markus Krajewski, "The Great Lightbulb Conspiracy," IEEE Spectrum, 2014; original cartel documentation in the Osram corporate archives)
- **Apple deliberately throttled older iPhone performance via iOS updates; fined 25 million euros by France, 10 million euros by Italy, and $113 million in U.S. class action settlement** (DGCCRF enforcement action, 2020; AGCM ruling, 2018; U.S. District Court settlement, 2020)
- **Global fashion industry produces over 100 billion garments per year; the average garment is worn only 7 times before disposal** (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, "A New Textiles Economy," 2017; McKinsey & Company, 2016)
- **Over 50 million metric tons of e-waste generated globally per year; only 17.4% formally recycled; fastest-growing waste stream on Earth** (UN Global E-waste Monitor, 2020)
- **John Deere tractor software locks prevent independent repair, requiring proprietary dealer access for diagnostics and servicing of $400,000+ equipment** (WIRED, 2020; U.S. Copyright Office DMCA exemption proceedings, 2015-2021)
- **U.S. vocational education enrollment dropped 14% between 1990 and 2009; many school districts eliminated shop, woodworking, and textile classes entirely** (National Center for Education Statistics, 2013; U.S. Department of Education Career and Technical Education data)
- **IKEA sells approximately 100 million pieces of furniture annually; internal testing acknowledges most products cannot survive a second disassembly** (IKEA Sustainability Report, 2020; BBC investigation, 2019)
- **The global shoe industry produces approximately 24 billion pairs per year; an estimated 300 million pairs are discarded annually in the U.S. alone, 95% ending in landfills** (World Footwear Yearbook, 2023; American Apparel & Footwear Association)
- **France's 2015 planned obsolescence law made it a criminal offense to deliberately shorten product lifespan, punishable by up to 2 years in prison and 300,000 euros in fines -- the first such law globally** (French Consumer Code, Article L441-2; European Parliament study on planned obsolescence, 2016)
- **The European Union's 2021 "right to repair" regulations require manufacturers of appliances to make spare parts available for up to 10 years and to design products for easier disassembly** (EU Ecodesign Directive implementing measures, 2021)
- **The average American household discards approximately 80 pounds of clothing per year; globally, one garbage truck of textiles is landfilled or burned every second** (EPA Office of Solid Waste data; Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2017)
- **Germany's dual education system maintains apprenticeship pathways for over 325 recognized trades, with approximately 1.3 million active apprentices; Germany remains the world's fourth-largest economy and a leader in precision manufacturing** (German Federal Institute for Vocational Education and Training, 2023)
- **The U.S. repair economy was valued at approximately $8.4 billion in 2022, a fraction of the $1.4 trillion consumer electronics market it serves, illustrating the scale of the imbalance between disposal and repair** (IBISWorld, 2022; Consumer Technology Association)

## Open Questions

- If planned obsolescence were criminalized globally (as France began doing in 2015), which industries would face the most structural disruption -- and what economic transition mechanisms would be required to prevent mass unemployment in disposability-dependent supply chains?
- Can the right-to-repair movement succeed at scale when manufacturers control both hardware design and software ecosystems, or will repair legislation be rendered symbolic by increasingly integrated, sealed, and software-locked product architectures?
- What would it take to restore apprenticeship and craft education to the center of public schooling, and what resistance would such a shift face from institutions invested in the college-preparation pipeline and the standardized testing industry?
- How does the disposability mindset extend into non-material domains -- relationships, communities, commitments -- and what evidence exists for the psychological link between consumer disposability conditioning and broader patterns of social disconnection?
- If Germany's dual education system demonstrates that robust craft training is compatible with economic competitiveness, what specifically prevented other industrialized nations from adopting similar models, and whose interests were served by their failure to do so?
