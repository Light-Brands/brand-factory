# Intellectual Property & Knowledge Enclosure -- Fencing the Commons of the Mind

_Part of the [Systemic Domination](../../../README.md) thesis._

## Content

### Opening Hook

On the night of January 6, 2011, a 24-year-old programmer named Aaron Swartz walked into an unlocked closet at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, connected a laptop to a network switch, and began downloading academic journal articles from JSTOR -- a digital archive of research papers produced almost entirely by publicly funded scientists and reviewed by unpaid academic volunteers. He downloaded approximately 4.8 million articles. JSTOR itself declined to prosecute. MIT equivocated. But the U.S. Attorney's office in Boston charged Swartz with 13 felony counts carrying a maximum sentence of 35 years in prison and $1 million in fines -- for downloading research papers that the public had already paid to produce. The prosecution offered a plea deal: six months in federal prison. Swartz refused. On January 11, 2013, at the age of 26, Aaron Swartz hanged himself in his Brooklyn apartment. The knowledge he tried to liberate remains behind paywalls. The message sent by his prosecution was unmistakable: the enclosure of publicly funded knowledge is a property right, and challenging it is a crime more aggressively punished than the fraud that crashed the global economy five years earlier.

Swartz's story illuminates one edge of a system that reaches much further. In 1999, a Canadian canola farmer named Percy Schmeiser discovered that Monsanto's patented Roundup Ready genetic modification had contaminated his fields through wind-borne pollen drift from neighboring farms. He had not purchased Monsanto seeds, had not planted them, and had not wanted them. Monsanto sued him anyway -- and won. The Supreme Court of Canada ruled that it did not matter how the patented genetic material arrived in his fields; its mere presence constituted patent infringement. A farmer was held liable for the behavior of wind. Meanwhile, in 2015, Martin Shkreli acquired the rights to Daraprim -- a 62-year-old drug used to treat parasitic infections in AIDS patients and pregnant women -- and raised the price from $13.50 per tablet to $750 overnight, a 5,455% increase. The drug was not new. No new research had been conducted. No new investment had been made. A man simply acquired a piece of paper that said he owned a molecule, and used it to extract wealth from people who would die without it. Three stories -- an activist prosecuted for freeing research, a farmer sued for the behavior of pollen, a profiteer exploiting a sick population through a patent on a decades-old drug -- and each reveals the same underlying architecture: the enclosure of knowledge, life, and nature itself as private property.

### The Architecture

For most of human history, knowledge was a commons. Techniques for growing food, building shelter, treating illness, and understanding the natural world were transmitted orally, refined collectively, and held by communities as shared inheritance. No one owned the idea of fermenting grain, or the knowledge that willow bark reduced fever, or the mathematics of the arch. Knowledge accumulated across generations precisely because it flowed freely. The modern intellectual property regime has enclosed this commons as thoroughly as the English Enclosure Acts fenced the physical land between the fifteenth and nineteenth centuries -- and with consequences at least as far-reaching.

The architecture of knowledge enclosure operates through four interlocking legal instruments: patents, copyrights, trade secrets, and trademarks. Each was originally conceived as a limited, temporary incentive for creation and invention. Each has been expanded, extended, and weaponized to the point where it now functions primarily as a mechanism for extracting rent from knowledge that, in many cases, was produced collectively, funded publicly, or discovered in nature. The U.S. patent system, established by the Constitution to "promote the progress of science and useful arts" through temporary monopolies, now grants approximately 400,000 patents per year, many of them so broad or abstract that they function as toll booths on entire fields of activity rather than protections for specific inventions. Patent trolls -- entities that produce nothing but acquire patent portfolios solely to extract licensing fees through threatened litigation -- now account for over 60% of all patent lawsuits in the United States, costing the economy an estimated $29 billion per year in direct litigation costs and substantially more in suppressed innovation, abandoned projects, and startup companies that never form because the patent landscape is too dangerous to navigate.

Copyright law has undergone a parallel expansion. The original Copyright Act of 1790 provided protection for 14 years, renewable once for a total of 28 years. Today, copyright lasts for the life of the author plus 70 years -- a span that can exceed 150 years from the date of creation. This expansion did not happen organically. The Copyright Term Extension Act of 1998 -- colloquially known as the "Mickey Mouse Protection Act" because it was lobbied into existence by the Walt Disney Company to prevent Mickey Mouse from entering the public domain -- added 20 years to existing copyright terms. The public domain, which once received a steady flow of works whose temporary protection had expired, has been virtually frozen since 1998. Works that would have been freely available to artists, educators, scholars, and the public remain privately controlled. The DMCA (Digital Millennium Copyright Act), passed the same year, criminalized even the act of circumventing digital locks on copyrighted material, making it a federal crime to bypass encryption on a work you legally purchased in order to use it in ways the copyright holder did not anticipate. The law does not merely protect content; it criminalizes the tools of access.

The academic publishing system represents perhaps the most brazen enclosure within the architecture. Elsevier, Springer Nature, and Wiley -- the three largest academic publishers -- maintain profit margins between 30% and 40%, rivaling or exceeding those of the most profitable technology companies on Earth. Their product is produced almost entirely by publicly funded researchers who receive no payment for their articles, peer-reviewed by academics who receive no payment for their reviews, and sold back to the universities whose faculty produced the work in the first place. The annual cost of academic journal subscriptions for a major research university can exceed $10 million. Researchers in the Global South are often entirely priced out of access to the scientific literature their own colleagues contributed to. The system extracts rent from every direction simultaneously: public funding produces the knowledge, unpaid labor reviews it, and institutions that already paid for its creation must pay again to read it. Sci-Hub, founded by Kazakhstani researcher Alexandra Elbakyan in 2011, provides free access to over 85 million academic papers and is used by researchers worldwide, including in wealthy nations whose universities cannot afford comprehensive journal access. It operates outside the law because the law protects the enclosure.

The international dimension of knowledge enclosure operates primarily through the TRIPS Agreement (Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights), imposed through the World Trade Organization as a condition of participation in the global trading system. TRIPS requires developing nations to adopt and enforce Western-style intellectual property protections, effectively preventing them from producing generic versions of patented medications, seeds, and technologies. During the COVID-19 pandemic, TRIPS protections initially prevented developing nations from manufacturing generic versions of vaccines developed with substantial public funding. India and South Africa's request for a temporary TRIPS waiver was blocked for over a year by the United States, the European Union, and Switzerland -- home to the largest pharmaceutical and agribusiness firms. The message was clear: intellectual property rights supersede the right to survive a pandemic.

### Key Mechanisms of Control

- **Pharmaceutical Patent Evergreening.** Drug companies make trivial modifications to existing medications -- a new coating, a different release mechanism, a mirror-image molecule -- and file new patents that extend monopoly pricing for decades beyond the original patent's expiration. Humira, the world's best-selling drug, was protected by over 100 patents filed by AbbVie, creating a "patent thicket" that delayed biosimilar competition for years while generating over $200 billion in cumulative revenue.

- **Patent Trolls and Litigation as Extraction.** Non-practicing entities that produce nothing acquire broad patents and use threatened litigation to extract licensing fees from companies that actually create products. Over 60% of patent lawsuits in the U.S. are filed by patent trolls, costing the economy an estimated $29 billion per year and functioning as a tax on innovation that disproportionately burdens small companies and startups.

- **Copyright Extension and Public Domain Freezing.** The Copyright Term Extension Act of 1998, lobbied by Disney, extended copyright to life-plus-70-years, effectively freezing the public domain. Works that should have been freely available for decades remain privately controlled, and the DMCA criminalizes circumventing digital locks even on legally purchased material.

- **Academic Publishing Paywalls.** Elsevier, Springer Nature, and Wiley maintain 30-40% profit margins on work produced by publicly funded researchers who are not paid for articles, reviewed by academics who are not paid for reviews, and sold back to the institutions that produced it. A single university can pay $10 million or more per year for journal access.

- **Seed Patents and Genetic Enclosure.** Monsanto/Bayer, Corteva, Syngenta, and BASF control approximately 60% of the global proprietary seed market. Seed patents prohibit farmers from saving and replanting seeds -- a practice as old as agriculture itself -- and contamination lawsuits hold farmers liable for wind-blown genetic material they never wanted. The ancient commons of seed-sharing has been enclosed within a single generation.

- **TRIPS Agreement and Global Enforcement.** The WTO's Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights agreement forces developing nations to adopt Western IP protections, preventing generic drug production, restricting technology transfer, and ensuring that the fruits of human knowledge flow from the Global South to corporate coffers in the Global North.

- **Biopiracy and Traditional Knowledge Theft.** Corporations patent compounds derived from indigenous knowledge -- neem oil, turmeric, ayahuasca, rooibos -- without consent, compensation, or acknowledgment. Traditional communities that maintained this knowledge for centuries are then required to license it back or face infringement claims on their own heritage.

- **Trade Secret Protections.** Trade secret law allows corporations to suppress information about harmful products, processes, and formulations by classifying them as proprietary. PFAS manufacturers, pesticide companies, and food processors have used trade secret claims to prevent public disclosure of health-relevant information for decades.

- **The Aaron Swartz Doctrine.** The aggressive prosecution of individuals who attempt to liberate enclosed knowledge sends a message that transcends any single case: knowledge liberation is treated as a more serious crime than financial fraud, environmental destruction, or corporate manslaughter. The deterrent effect is the point.

- **Software Patents and Digital Enclosure.** Broad software patents -- on concepts like "one-click purchasing," "slide to unlock," or "using a computer to do something previously done on paper" -- function as toll booths on digital activity, allowing large firms to extract licensing fees from or litigate against competitors, particularly small developers who cannot afford patent defense.

- **Data Enclosure.** The emerging frontier of knowledge enclosure treats the behavioral data generated by billions of human beings as the proprietary raw material of surveillance capitalism. Your movements, preferences, relationships, and habits are collected without meaningful consent, processed into predictive models, and sold -- but you have no ownership stake in the knowledge extracted from your own life.

### Independent Conditioning

The intellectual property regime conditions human consciousness by naturalizing the idea that knowledge can be owned -- a concept that, examined from outside the system, is as strange as claiming to own gravity or the color blue. Every child who encounters a paywall, a "this content is not available in your region" notice, or a copyright warning is being taught that knowledge is a commodity rather than a commons. Every researcher who cannot access a study in their own field because their institution cannot afford the subscription is learning that understanding is rationed by wealth. Every farmer who cannot save seed from their own harvest is absorbing the lesson that life itself can be enclosed and that the most fundamental acts of agriculture -- selecting, saving, replanting -- now require corporate permission.

This conditioning operates at the level of imagination. When knowledge is enclosed, the range of what people can think, create, and discover is artificially constrained. A musician who cannot freely sample, remix, and build on existing works without navigating a licensing labyrinth is not merely inconvenienced; their creative field has been narrowed. A medical researcher in Lagos who cannot read the literature relevant to a disease devastating their community is not merely underserved; they have been structurally excluded from the global conversation about healing. A student who learns that academic knowledge comes with a price tag absorbs a lesson more fundamental than any course content: understanding is for those who can afford it. The system does not need to explicitly say "you do not deserve to know." The paywall says it silently, automatically, at scale.

The deepest damage is to the collective. Human knowledge is, by its nature, cumulative and collaborative. Newton stood on the shoulders of giants; those giants stood on the shoulders of others, back through millennia of shared discovery. Every patent, every copyright extension, every paywall interrupts this accumulation by inserting a toll booth into the chain of human understanding. The aggregate effect is incalculable: the inventions never made because the relevant prior art was locked behind a patent thicket, the diseases whose cures were delayed because the foundational research was behind a paywall, the cultural works never created because the raw material of previous human expression was proprietary. The intellectual property regime does not merely enclose what exists; it forecloses what could have been. It narrows the future by fencing the past.

### Interlocking with Other Systems

The intellectual property system interlocks with nearly every other mechanism of control in this thesis because knowledge is the substrate of all human activity -- and enclosing it grants leverage over every domain that depends on understanding.

The most lethal interlock is with the Medical-Pharmaceutical Complex (Section 05). Patent monopolies are the mechanism through which pharmaceutical companies maintain prices that bear no relationship to production costs. Insulin costs approximately $5 to manufacture and sells for up to $300 per vial in the United States. The patent system does not protect the inventor of insulin -- that was Frederick Banting, who sold the patent in 1923 for one dollar because he believed no one should profit from a discovery necessary to save lives. The patent system protects the corporations that acquired, modified, and re-patented insulin delivery mechanisms to extend monopoly pricing indefinitely. Evergreening, patent thickets, and "pay-for-delay" agreements with generic manufacturers cost the U.S. healthcare system an estimated $30 billion per year. People die because knowledge that could save them is enclosed.

The Financial System & Capitalism (Section 23) has transformed intellectual property from a legal protection into a financial asset class. IP portfolios are valued on corporate balance sheets, used as collateral for loans, traded as securities, and managed by specialized investment funds. When Nortel Networks went bankrupt in 2011, its patent portfolio sold for $4.5 billion -- more than the company's tangible assets were worth. Knowledge has become a speculative instrument, valued not for what it teaches but for what it can extract. Biotech & Genetic Control (Section 10) depends on intellectual property law to enclose life itself. Seed patents, gene patents, and CRISPR technology licenses determine who may use the building blocks of biology and on what terms. The four largest agribusiness firms control approximately 60% of the global proprietary seed market, and patent law is the mechanism that prevents farmers from doing what farmers have done for 10,000 years: saving seed.

The Education System (Section 14) is both a victim and a vector of knowledge enclosure. Academic paywalls restrict access to the research that education depends on, while universities themselves have become IP generation engines, filing patents on faculty research and licensing discoveries to corporations rather than releasing them to the public that funded them. The Bayh-Dole Act of 1980, which allowed universities to patent publicly funded research, transformed academic culture from knowledge-sharing to knowledge-enclosing within a generation. The Suppression of Sacred Science (Section 49) represents the deepest layer of knowledge enclosure: ancient healing systems, energy technologies, consciousness research, and ecological wisdom traditions are not merely paywalled but actively suppressed, dismissed as pseudoscience, and excluded from institutional funding -- not because they lack efficacy but because they cannot be patented and therefore cannot generate returns within the IP-dependent economic model.

Colonialism & Neo-Colonialism (Section 43) operates through intellectual property as one of its primary contemporary mechanisms. TRIPS forces developing nations to adopt IP regimes designed for wealthy economies, biopiracy extracts traditional knowledge from indigenous communities and converts it into patented corporate property, and the global geography of patent ownership mirrors the global geography of colonial extraction almost exactly. The Media (Section 15) enforces the normalization of knowledge enclosure through copyright culture -- training populations to accept that sharing is "piracy," that access requires payment, and that the free flow of information is theft rather than its natural state. Modern Government (Section 38) provides the enforcement mechanism: patent offices, copyright registries, courts, and international trade agreements all exist to protect the enclosure. The Liberation Architecture (Section 58) points toward the counter-movement: open-source software, Creative Commons licensing, Sci-Hub, the open-access publishing movement, seed libraries, and indigenous knowledge sovereignty efforts that seek to re-common what has been enclosed.

### Sacred Laws Violated

The intellectual property regime constitutes a systematic violation of Sacred Laws governing the relationship between human beings and knowledge itself.

The **Law of Sacred Knowledge** holds that wisdom and understanding are the shared inheritance of all beings and that their suppression or enclosure is a violation of cosmic order. Knowledge is not a commodity that diminishes when shared; it is the one resource that multiplies through distribution. Every paywall, every patent thicket, every prosecution of knowledge liberators violates this law by treating the infinite as scarce and the communal as private. The **Law of Abundance** is inverted when the IP system manufactures artificial scarcity around knowledge that could, at zero marginal cost, be available to every human being on Earth. A research paper exists as a digital file that can be copied infinitely at effectively no cost. The paywall does not protect a scarce resource; it creates scarcity where none naturally exists. This is not intellectual property "protection" -- it is abundance destruction.

The **Law of Community** is violated when knowledge enclosure fragments the collective capacity to learn, heal, create, and solve problems together. When a researcher in Nairobi cannot read the same papers as a researcher in Boston, the human community is not merely unequal -- it is broken. The chain of understanding that connects all seekers across time and space is interrupted at every toll booth. And the **Law of Sovereignty** is violated in its deepest sense when farmers cannot save their own seed, when communities cannot use their own traditional knowledge without corporate permission, and when nations cannot manufacture generic medicines to save their own people. Intellectual property law, at its most aggressive, claims sovereignty over life processes, molecular structures, and genetic sequences -- asserting ownership not merely over human creations but over the patterns of nature itself.

## Open Questions

- What would happen to global scientific progress if all publicly funded research were required to be published in open access -- and what political strategies could overcome the combined lobbying power of Elsevier, Springer Nature, and Wiley?
- Is the concept of "intellectual property" coherent at all, or does it conflate fundamentally different phenomena (inventions, creative works, trade secrets, brands) under a single framework that serves enclosure rather than creation?
- How do we construct a global framework for protecting indigenous traditional knowledge that does not itself become another form of enclosure -- recognizing communal knowledge without forcing it into the individualized ownership model of Western IP law?
- What would a patent system designed to genuinely promote innovation look like, and how short would patent terms need to be to incentivize creation without enabling the rent-seeking extraction that currently dominates?
- If knowledge is the one resource that multiplies through sharing, what economic models can sustain knowledge creation without relying on artificial scarcity -- and what can we learn from open-source software, which has produced some of the most important technological infrastructure in history without patent protection?
