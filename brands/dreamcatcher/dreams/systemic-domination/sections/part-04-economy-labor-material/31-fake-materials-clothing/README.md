# Fake Materials & Synthetic Clothing -- Wrapping the Body in Plastic

_Part of the [Systemic Domination](../../../README.md) thesis._

## Content

### Opening Hook

In March 2022, a research team at Vrije Universiteit Amsterdam published the first study detecting microplastic particles in human blood. Of the 22 healthy volunteers tested, 17 -- nearly eighty percent -- had quantifiable concentrations of plastic in their bloodstreams. The most common polymer found was PET, the plastic used to make polyester clothing and beverage bottles. The second most common was polystyrene, used in food packaging. The researchers noted that these particles had crossed the gut or lung barrier, entered the circulatory system, and could be transported to every organ in the body. The study was peer-reviewed and published in Environment International. It confirmed what environmental scientists had been warning about for years: plastic is no longer merely an environmental pollutant. It is inside us. It is in human blood, in lung tissue, in placentas, in breast milk, in the brains of the living and the organs of the dead. And one of the primary delivery systems is the fabric we wear against our skin every hour of every day.

Five years earlier, on April 24, 2013, the Rana Plaza building in the Savar district of Dhaka, Bangladesh, collapsed during morning working hours, killing 1,134 garment workers and injuring over 2,500. The building housed five garment factories producing clothing for Western brands including Primark, Benetton, Walmart, and The Children's Place. The day before the collapse, cracks had been discovered in the building's structure. A bank and shops on the lower floors evacuated immediately. The garment workers were ordered to return to their sewing machines under threat of having a month's wages docked. The building came down at 8:57 the next morning. It was the deadliest structural failure in modern industrial history and the deadliest disaster in the garment industry's existence. The brands whose products were being manufactured inside paid settlements amounting to pennies per garment sold. Within a year, global fast fashion production had increased. These two data points -- microplastics in blood, workers buried in rubble -- mark the two poles of the synthetic materials system: what it does to the bodies that wear the products, and what it does to the bodies that make them.

### The Architecture

The transformation of human clothing from natural to synthetic materials is one of the most consequential material shifts in the history of civilization, and it occurred in less than a single human lifetime. In 1960, synthetic fibers accounted for less than ten percent of global textile production. By 2023, polyester alone accounted for over fifty-four percent, with total synthetics -- polyester, nylon, acrylic, spandex, and their blends -- exceeding sixty-five percent. This shift was not driven by consumer preference or superior performance. It was driven by a single variable: cost. Polyester can be produced for roughly half the price per kilogram of cotton. It is derived from petroleum -- specifically from terephthalic acid and ethylene glycol, both petrochemical products -- linking the clothing on your back to the same fossil fuel infrastructure that drives the energy monopoly and the climate crisis. The global polyester industry produces approximately 60 million tons per year, consuming an estimated 70 million barrels of oil annually. When you wear a polyester shirt, you are wearing petroleum. When you wash it, you are shedding petroleum into the water supply. When you discard it, you are burying petroleum in the ground, where it will persist for centuries.

The health implications of wrapping the body's largest organ in petroleum derivatives are only beginning to be documented, because the shift occurred before the research did. Synthetic fabrics are treated with a chemical arsenal designed to make cheap fabric behave like quality material: formaldehyde resins for wrinkle resistance, PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances, "forever chemicals") for stain and water resistance, azo dyes linked to dermatitis and potential carcinogenicity, antimicrobial treatments containing triclosan and silver nanoparticles whose effects on the skin microbiome are almost entirely unstudied, and flame retardants -- particularly in children's sleepwear -- that have been linked to endocrine disruption. Unlike natural fibers, synthetics do not breathe. They trap heat and moisture against the skin, creating microenvironments favorable to bacterial and fungal overgrowth. A 2011 study published in the Journal of Textile Science & Engineering found that polyester garments harbored significantly more odor-causing bacteria than cotton garments, because bacteria adhere more readily to petroleum-based surfaces. The body was not designed to be sealed in plastic, yet the majority of clothing now available at any price point below luxury contains a majority synthetic composition.

The fast fashion industry is the delivery vehicle for this material transformation. Producing over 100 billion garments annually for 8 billion people, the industry operates on a model of radical overproduction, manufacturing roughly twelve garments per person per year, the vast majority designed to be worn fewer than ten times before disposal. Brands like Shein -- which adds an estimated 2,000 to 10,000 new styles to its platform daily -- have compressed the fashion cycle from four seasonal collections to an essentially continuous stream of disposable novelty. The environmental toll is staggering: the fashion industry generates approximately ten percent of global carbon emissions, consumes 93 billion cubic meters of water annually, and sends an estimated 92 million tons of textile waste to landfills each year. Synthetic garments do not biodegrade. They fragment into progressively smaller plastic particles that persist in the environment for 200 to 500 years. A single load of laundry containing synthetic garments releases between 700,000 and 1.9 million microplastic fibers into waterways per wash cycle -- fibers too small to be captured by most wastewater treatment plants. These fibers have now been documented in every ocean basin, in Arctic ice cores, in agricultural soils, in drinking water, in table salt, and in the tissues of marine organisms from plankton to whales.

The human cost is concentrated, as it always is, in the Global South. Bangladesh, Vietnam, Cambodia, Myanmar, and Ethiopia host the garment factories that produce the world's cheapest clothing. Workers in these facilities -- predominantly women, often as young as fourteen -- labor for wages that rarely exceed two to three dollars per day, under conditions that include forced overtime, exposure to chemical dyes and finishing agents without protective equipment, and buildings that do not meet structural safety codes. The Rana Plaza collapse was not an aberration. It was the visible moment in an invisible system. The International Labour Organization has estimated that over 60 million people work in the global garment industry, the majority in conditions that meet internationally recognized definitions of labor exploitation. The brands whose labels appear on the garments maintain their distance through layers of subcontracting that create plausible deniability: the company contracts with a factory, the factory subcontracts to a smaller workshop, the workshop employs piece-rate workers in conditions the brand has never inspected and claims no responsibility for. The supply chain is designed to be opaque, because transparency would reveal what cheap clothing actually costs.

### Key Mechanisms of Control

- **Petrochemical Fiber Dependency** -- Over 65% of global textile production now uses petroleum-derived synthetic fibers, linking the clothing industry directly to the fossil fuel supply chain. Polyester production consumes approximately 70 million barrels of oil per year. This dependency ensures that any transition away from synthetics threatens the revenue streams of some of the world's most powerful corporations -- ExxonMobil, Shell, SABIC, Reliance Industries -- whose petrochemical divisions produce the raw materials from which synthetic fibers are spun.

- **Microplastic Contamination** -- Each wash cycle of synthetic clothing releases 700,000 to 1.9 million microplastic fibers into waterways. These particles are too small for most wastewater treatment systems to capture. They accumulate in oceans, soils, drinking water, food chains, and human tissues -- blood, lungs, placentas, breast milk. The contamination is now essentially global and irreversible within any human timeframe. A 2023 meta-analysis estimated that the average person ingests approximately five grams of microplastic per week -- roughly the weight of a credit card.

- **Chemical Finishing Cocktail** -- Synthetic garments are treated with formaldehyde, PFAS, azo dyes, antimicrobial agents, flame retardants, and softening compounds whose combined and cumulative health effects have never been studied in longitudinal human trials. These chemicals are absorbed through dermal contact, inhalation of off-gassing, and ingestion of shed fibers. The precautionary principle is inverted: chemicals are assumed safe until proven harmful, and the burden of proof falls on injured populations, not on manufacturers.

- **Fast Fashion Overproduction** -- The industry produces over 100 billion garments annually, roughly twelve per person on Earth. Brands like Shein add thousands of new styles daily. The average garment is worn seven times before disposal. This radical overproduction generates 92 million tons of textile waste per year, the vast majority synthetic and therefore non-biodegradable. The model depends on garments being cheap enough to be effectively disposable -- which requires the cheapest possible materials, the cheapest possible labor, and the lowest possible environmental standards.

- **Global South Labor Exploitation** -- Over 60 million garment workers, predominantly women, labor in conditions that include sub-living wages, forced overtime, chemical exposure, and structurally unsafe buildings. Supply chain opacity through layers of subcontracting creates plausible deniability for Western brands. The Rana Plaza disaster killed 1,134 workers; within a year, global production volumes had increased.

- **Greenwashing "Sustainable" Synthetics** -- "Recycled polyester" is marketed as an environmental solution while it still sheds microplastics, still requires energy-intensive processing, and still does not biodegrade. The recycling rate of textiles globally is approximately 12%, and recycled polyester typically downcycles -- it can be recycled once or twice before becoming waste. "Ocean plastic" clothing campaigns convert a tiny fraction of marine debris into garments that shed new microplastics with every wash, potentially accelerating the problem they claim to solve.

- **Natural Fiber Suppression** -- Hemp, once one of the world's most versatile and widely cultivated fibers, was effectively banned in the United States from 1937 until the 2018 Farm Bill, a prohibition driven not by the plant's psychoactive properties (industrial hemp contains negligible THC) but by its threat to the cotton, timber, and petrochemical industries. Organic cotton farming receives a fraction of the subsidies given to conventional cotton, which itself receives a fraction of the subsidies flowing to petrochemical production. The market is structurally tilted against the natural fibers that would reduce both microplastic pollution and chemical exposure.

- **Vinyl and PVC in the Home** -- Beyond clothing, synthetic materials dominate the built environment: vinyl flooring, PVC piping, synthetic carpet, polyurethane foam in mattresses and furniture, melamine-coated particleboard. These materials off-gas volatile organic compounds (VOCs) including formaldehyde, toluene, and phthalates, creating indoor air quality that the EPA has found to be two to five times more polluted than outdoor air. The average person spends 90% of their time indoors, marinating in a petrochemical atmosphere.

- **BPA, Phthalates, and Endocrine Disruption** -- Synthetic materials used in food packaging, beverage containers, children's toys, and personal care products contain endocrine-disrupting chemicals that mimic or interfere with human hormones. BPA exposure has been linked to reproductive disorders, obesity, cardiovascular disease, and neurodevelopmental effects. Phthalates, used as plasticizers in PVC and as fragrance carriers, are detectable in the urine of over 97% of the U.S. population. These are not trace contaminants. They are ubiquitous design features of the synthetic material economy.

- **Synthetic Food Additives** -- The same petrochemical infrastructure that produces synthetic textiles produces synthetic food dyes, preservatives, emulsifiers, and flavoring agents. Red Dye No. 40, Yellow No. 5, and similar petroleum-derived colorants are consumed in vast quantities, particularly by children, despite evidence linking them to hyperactivity and behavioral effects. The European Union requires warning labels on foods containing these dyes; the United States does not.

### Independent Conditioning

The synthetic material system, operating without any other system of control, would still fundamentally reshape human consciousness by severing the body's relationship with the natural world at its most intimate point of contact: the skin. For most of human history, the materials that touched the body -- clothing, bedding, shelter -- were drawn from living systems. Cotton was grown, wool was shorn, linen was harvested, leather was tanned. These materials carried the texture and temperature of the living world. They breathed with the body, aged with the wearer, and returned to the earth when their useful life ended. The replacement of these materials with petroleum derivatives does not merely change what clothing is made of. It changes the body's relationship with the material world itself. A person dressed in polyester, sleeping on polyurethane foam, walking on vinyl flooring, eating from plastic containers, and drinking from plastic bottles exists in a petrochemical cocoon that interposes a layer of dead matter between the living body and the living earth at every point of contact.

This material severance has psychological and spiritual dimensions that extend beyond the physical. When the objects of daily life are synthetic -- when they carry no history, no texture of origin, no connection to plant or animal or craft tradition -- they teach the body, at a level below conscious thought, that the material world is inert, fungible, and meaningless. Natural materials carry information: the grain of wood tells the story of the tree's growth; the weave of linen records the rhythm of the loom; the patina of leather maps the life of the wearer. Synthetic materials carry nothing. They are uniform, anonymous, and disposable. A civilization surrounded by such materials develops a corresponding relationship with materiality itself -- one of indifference, disconnection, and the assumption that physical reality is something to be managed rather than inhabited with care.

The normalization of synthetic exposure also conditions populations to accept chemical contamination as a background condition of modern life rather than an engineered outcome. When microplastics are found in blood, the response is not outrage but resignation. When PFAS are found in drinking water, communities are told to wait for more data. The slow saturation of the human body with industrial chemistry produces not alarm but acclimation -- a learned helplessness in the face of contamination that serves the interests of the industries producing it. If the contamination is everywhere, it seems to come from nowhere, and if it comes from nowhere, no one is responsible.

### Interlocking with Other Systems

The synthetic material system does not operate in isolation. It is embedded in a network of industrial, financial, and political systems that collectively make petrochemical textiles the default and natural alternatives the exception.

Ecocide (Section 32) receives the physical output: microplastic pollution now contaminates every ocean, every continent, and the tissues of virtually every species studied. The 92 million tons of textile waste generated annually -- predominantly synthetic and non-biodegradable -- represent one of the fastest-growing contributions to planetary contamination. The fashion industry's ten percent share of global carbon emissions links clothing directly to climate change. Cheap Quality Over Craftsmanship (Section 30) operates as the companion mechanism: planned obsolescence determines that garments will be disposable, and synthetic materials ensure they are cheap enough to discard without financial pain and persistent enough to remain in the environment for centuries. The two systems are mutually reinforcing -- cheapness demands synthetics, and synthetics enable cheapness.

The Medical-Pharmaceutical Complex (Section 05) stands at the receiving end of the health consequences: endocrine disruption from BPA and phthalates, respiratory effects from microplastic inhalation, dermatological reactions to chemical finishes, and the as-yet-unknown long-term effects of microplastic accumulation in blood and organs. The healthcare system treats these conditions as individual medical problems rather than as symptoms of industrial contamination, generating pharmaceutical revenue from problems whose root cause is material. Colonialism & Neo-Colonialism (Section 43) provides the labor geography: Bangladesh, Cambodia, Vietnam, Myanmar, and Ethiopia serve as the manufacturing floor for fast fashion, their workers exploited under conditions that reproduce the colonial extraction pattern -- labor and resources flow out, profit flows back to the metropole. Beauty & Body Image (Section 13) drives the consumption engine: the fashion cycle's relentless production of new trends and the social media amplification of appearance-based status ensure that clothing is consumed not for need but for identity performance, generating demand that only the fastest, cheapest, most synthetic production model can satisfy.

The Financial System & Capitalism (Section 23) provides the structural logic: the extractive business model that governs fast fashion -- externalize labor costs, externalize environmental costs, maximize throughput, capture quarterly earnings -- is the same model that governs the financial system itself. The Energy Monopoly (Section 34) provides the raw material: polyester is petroleum, and the petrochemical industry's diversification into textiles creates a second revenue stream from the same fossil fuel extraction infrastructure. Suppression of Sacred Science (Section 49) provides the historical context: hemp prohibition, the marginalization of natural fiber research, and the suppression of traditional textile knowledge all cleared the field for petrochemical dominance. Counter-Movements & Resistance (Section 57) offers the nascent response: the slow fashion movement, organic textile certification, microplastic filtration technology, and garment worker organizing represent early-stage resistance to the synthetic material system.

### Sacred Laws Violated

The synthetic material system violates sacred principles at the most intimate level -- the boundary between the body and the world, the surface through which we touch and are touched by material reality.

The **Law of Reverence for Life** is violated when the materials closest to the living body are derived from dead matter -- petroleum extracted from ancient geological deposits, processed through industrial chemistry, and shaped into fabrics that neither breathe nor biodegrade. To wrap the body in plastic is to sever its relationship with the living material world at every point of contact. The natural fibers that clothed humanity for millennia were themselves alive or recently alive -- cotton from a plant, wool from an animal, linen from flax, silk from a cocoon. They carried the life force of their origins. Synthetic fibers carry nothing but chemistry. The **Law of Healing** is violated when the materials that surround the body introduce harm rather than support: endocrine-disrupting chemicals absorbed through the skin, microplastics crossing into the bloodstream, volatile organic compounds off-gassing from furniture and flooring into the air of homes where children sleep. A material environment that slowly contaminates the bodies within it is the opposite of a healing environment, and a civilization that has normalized this contamination has normalized a slow form of poisoning. The **Law of Truth** is violated by the systematic deception embedded in the synthetic material economy: "recycled polyester" marketed as sustainable when it still sheds microplastics; "organic" labels on garments finished with PFAS; "fair trade" certifications that cover a fraction of the supply chain; ingredient lists on clothing that omit the chemical finishes applied after weaving. The consumer is denied the information needed to make an informed choice, and the marketing apparatus actively produces the illusion that toxic products are safe. The **Law of Interconnection** is violated by a material system that severs the connections between wearer and maker, between consumer and ecosystem, between the shirt on a rack and the worker who sewed it under duress, between the microplastic in the ocean and the polyester in the washing machine. The synthetic material economy is designed to be opaque, to hide its connections, to prevent the wearer from seeing the web of extraction, exploitation, and contamination that produced the garment on their body. To obscure interconnection is to deny it, and to deny it is to violate the sacred principle that all things are related.

## Evidence & Data

- **Microplastics detected in human blood in 80% of tested volunteers; PET (polyester plastic) was the most common polymer found** (Leslie et al., Environment International, 2022)
- **Rana Plaza collapse (April 24, 2013) killed 1,134 garment workers and injured 2,500+ in Dhaka, Bangladesh; workers had been ordered back into a building with visible structural cracks** (International Labour Organization report; Clean Clothes Campaign investigation, 2013)
- **A single wash of synthetic clothing releases 700,000 to 1.9 million microplastic fibers into waterways** (Napper & Thompson, Marine Pollution Bulletin, 2016; De Falco et al., Scientific Reports, 2019)
- **Polyester accounts for 54% of global fiber production; total synthetics exceed 65%; polyester production consumes approximately 70 million barrels of oil annually** (Textile Exchange Preferred Fiber and Materials Market Report, 2023)
- **Fashion industry generates approximately 10% of global carbon emissions, consumes 93 billion cubic meters of water annually, and sends 92 million tons of textile waste to landfills per year** (UNEP, 2019; Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2017)
- **Over 60 million people work in the global garment industry; median wage in Bangladesh garment sector: approximately $95/month, far below living wage estimates** (International Labour Organization; Clean Clothes Campaign, 2023)
- **PFAS ("forever chemicals") detected in clothing, including children's clothing, from major retail brands; compounds linked to cancer, thyroid disease, immune suppression, and reproductive harm** (Peaslee et al., Environmental Science & Technology Letters, 2022; Toxic-Free Future report, 2022)
- **Shein adds an estimated 2,000-10,000 new styles to its platform daily; valued at approximately $100 billion in 2023** (Bloomberg; Rest of World investigation, 2023)
- **Average person ingests approximately 5 grams of microplastic per week -- the weight of a credit card** (World Wildlife Fund / University of Newcastle meta-analysis, 2019)
- **Microplastics found in human placentas for the first time in 2020; subsequent studies confirmed presence in lung tissue, liver, and colon tissue** (Ragusa et al., Environment International, 2021; Jenner et al., Science of the Total Environment, 2022)
- **Hemp was effectively banned in the United States from 1937 (Marihuana Tax Act) until the 2018 Farm Bill, despite industrial hemp containing negligible THC; the ban protected cotton, timber, and petrochemical textile interests** (Herer, The Emperor Wears No Clothes; Congressional Research Service, 2019)
- **Indoor air quality is 2-5x more polluted than outdoor air, driven by off-gassing from synthetic materials in flooring, furniture, paint, and textiles** (EPA "Report on the Environment," updated 2023)
- **The global textile recycling rate is approximately 12%; "recycled polyester" typically downcycles and continues to shed microplastics** (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2017; Greenpeace "Fashion at the Crossroads" report)
- **Red Dye No. 40 and other petroleum-derived food colorants linked to behavioral effects in children; EU requires warning labels, the U.S. does not** (McCann et al., The Lancet, 2007; FDA advisory committee review, 2011)

## Open Questions

- Given that microplastic contamination is now detected in human blood, organs, and placentas, what longitudinal health studies would be required to establish causation between synthetic textile exposure and chronic disease -- and who would fund such studies when the industries responsible have no incentive to produce the data?
- Can the slow fashion and natural fiber movements achieve scale sufficient to challenge petrochemical textile dominance, or will they remain luxury niches accessible only to consumers who can afford to pay the true cost of clothing?
- What would a genuine reparations framework look like for garment workers in the Global South, given that the brands profiting from their exploitation operate through supply chain structures specifically designed to shield them from legal accountability?
- If hemp had never been prohibited, how different would the global textile landscape be today -- and what does the history of hemp suppression reveal about the mechanism by which superior natural alternatives are displaced by inferior synthetic ones to serve corporate interests?
- How do we address the psychological normalization of contamination -- the learned helplessness that treats microplastics in blood, PFAS in drinking water, and endocrine disruptors in children's clothing as inevitable features of modern life rather than as engineered outcomes that could be reversed?
