## Content

The thesis already addresses digital surveillance and AI oversight (Section 16), but social media warrants its own reckoning. Where surveillance is about watching, social media is about *shaping* -- an active engine of psychological manipulation operating at a scale unprecedented in human history. Sean Parker, co-founder of Facebook, publicly admitted in 2017 that the platform was designed to exploit "a vulnerability in human psychology," consuming as much user time and attention as possible by delivering "a little dopamine hit" with every like and comment. Tristan Harris, former Google design ethicist, has since catalogued how every major platform deploys persuasive design -- infinite scroll, autoplay, notification bombardment -- not as accidental features but as deliberate extraction tools. The average person now checks their phone over 96 times per day. Attention itself has become the commodity.

The consequences extend far beyond wasted hours. Polarization algorithms, tuned to maximize engagement, systematically amplify outrage because fury keeps eyes on screens. The result is an outrage economy where the most inflammatory content is the most profitable. Jonathan Haidt's research has documented a dramatic spike in adolescent anxiety, depression, and self-harm beginning around 2012 -- precisely when smartphone saturation and Instagram adoption crossed critical thresholds among teenagers. Comparison culture, in which curated highlight reels replace authentic human interaction, drives chronic insufficiency. Influencer culture has supplanted organic community with parasocial relationships -- millions of people forming one-directional emotional bonds with strangers who are, in turn, selling them products. Meanwhile, bot networks manipulate public discourse at industrial scale, manufacturing the illusion of consensus or dissent on demand. Cancel culture functions as a distributed system of social control: no central authority is needed when the crowd itself enforces ideological conformity through reputational destruction. The "dead internet" theory -- that a growing percentage of online activity is generated by bots rather than humans -- may be extreme, but the direction is unmistakable. What was once the digital public square is increasingly a managed simulation.

**Key mechanisms of control:**

- **Dopamine-driven persuasive design.** Variable reward schedules, infinite scroll, notification triggers.
- **Polarization algorithms.** Optimizing for engagement through outrage.
- **Comparison culture.** Curated self-presentation driving anxiety and depression.
- **Influencer economy.** Replacing authentic community with parasocial consumption.
- **Bot manipulation.** Public discourse and manufactured consensus.
- **Cancel culture.** Decentralized social enforcement.
- **Adolescent mental health crisis.** Linked to smartphone/social media saturation.
- **Attention harvesting.** The core business model -- the user is the product.
