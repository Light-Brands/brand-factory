# Corrupt Capitalism – Profit Over People and Planet

_Part of the [Systemic Domination](../../README.md) thesis._

---

## Content

“Corrupt capitalism” refers to a stage of the economic system where everything is driven by the bottom line, to the detriment of human values. In this model, people become products and nature mere input – all exploited for maximum short-term profit. One glaring example is how surveillance capitalism treats personal data: as scholar Shoshana Zuboff observes, tech companies today “unilaterally claim human experience as free raw material for translation into behavioral data.”goodreads.com In other words, our thoughts, preferences, and online behaviors are being mined and sold without our genuine consent. This commodification of every aspect of life raises profound ethical issues – privacy is eroded, and individuals are viewed not as citizens with rights, but as data points to be monetized. More broadly, late-stage capitalism has enabled large corporations to regard consumers and workers as nothing more than means to profit. If a service is free, you are the product being sold; if labor is cheaper overseas, domestic workers are laid off; if cutting safety corners boosts stock price, the risks to consumers or employees are ignored.

This relentless profit-first mentality also explains environmental and social harm on a grand scale. A striking statistic: just 100 companies have been the source of over 70% of the world’s industrial greenhouse gas emissions since 1988theguardian.com. These are mainly fossil fuel corporations whose business model, unchecked, has driven the climate crisis – an existential threat to humanity. Yet under corrupt capitalism, such companies long prioritized extracting and selling oil/gas (and lobbying to block climate action) because it was enormously profitable, regardless of the damage to the planet’s future. Similarly, pharmaceutical firms have at times concealed drug side-effects or aggressively pushed opioids for profit, even as it led to public health disasters. Food conglomerates advertise addictive, unhealthy foods because it’s lucrative, while obesity and diabetes rates surge. In each case, profit is privatized while costs are externalized onto society or the environment. Any attempt to regulate or reduce these harms is met with fierce resistance – because that might hurt the quarterly earnings.

Under this distorted system, human life and dignity become secondary to financial metrics. Workers are often seen as expendable costs: companies will lay off thousands to boost a stock’s value, or keep wages stagnant to enrich shareholders, reflecting a calculus that values money over livelihoods. Consumers are treated as targets for perpetual upselling and manipulation – consider how social media algorithms amplify outrage or anxiety, because keeping us hooked yields more ad revenue. Even democracy is undermined (as discussed) by corporate money ensuring policies favor profit-makers. In sum, corrupt capitalism reduces all interactions to transactions. It has commodified our data, our attention, our health, and even our relationships. The bottom line-driven mentality supports the grand distortion by normalizing exploitation: as long as it’s profitable, it’s justified. Of course, not all business is evil and profit motive can drive innovation, but without ethical and societal counterbalances, capitalism left to its own devices tends to devour the very social fabric and natural resources it depends on. Recognizing this truth is the first step to demanding a more responsible, people-centered economy that measures success in more than just dollars.
